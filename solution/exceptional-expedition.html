Solution: Exceptional Expedition
Answer: TOP-GRADE

Written by Abby Caron

We are presented with several pages from what appears to be a researcher’s journal. On a first pass, we might notice that they go to a different location each month, and at each, write a bit about their trip, describe some animals or plants, and then note a particularly odd animal that was seen on a specific day.

Identifying the odd animals at the end of each paragraph (perhaps starting with TRICERATOPS or ARCHEOPTERYX) it should be clear that they are all extinct creatures. They are presented in alphabetical order to aid in identification.

Indexing into the identified taxa by the day they were spotted in the order they are presented, we see the word “LAGERSTATTEN”. Recognizing this (or with a quick google) we know that Lagerstätten are sites of exceptional fossil preservation – and there are only a few known in the world.

Now we should be able to identify a Lagerstätte corresponding to each place visited. They are clued via the taxa that are found there, as well as the occasional pun, location, or reference. For example, January includes a reference to Santana (the Latin rock band with hit song “Smooth”), references to the ecology of the Santana biota (shallow inland sea, lagoons), and description of the fauna preserved there (bright crested pterosaurs, crocodylomorphs, etc). We can find one location to match each month, perhaps using the Wikipedia page on Lagerstätte to help. Note that the initial identified animals were always the exception - they do not belong to the same place or period as the Lagerstätte in the same month.

At this point, we should zoom out and look again at the journal entries. The first letters of each spell out “STENO AND MOHS” -- now that we know we are looking at a paleontology puzzle, those should hint at Steno's laws of stratigraphy and Mohs scale of mineral hardness.

Looking for minerals (for use with Mohs scale), we might initially see DIAMOND as the “clear carbon gem”. Looking harder, we can see some alternate names, such as “alabaster” for GYPSUM, or “rock crystal” for QUARTZ. Each corresponds to one of the ten standard minerals uniquely associated with a number 1 through 10 on Mohs scale. Furthermore, in the initial ordering, the sites are presented in ascending Mohs scale of mineral hardness order to aid with identification.

Ordering the Lagerstätten by geologic age (as clued by the reference to Nicholas Steno and his work on stratigraphy) and indexing by the associated Mohs scale number will give our final answer. But how to read it? We should remember the first law of stratigraphy – by the Law of Superposition, the first deposited rocks will be the deepest layer. So reading from the oldest strata to youngest we see TOPGRADEISIT; thus the “perfect way to describe my work this year” is our final answer: TOP-GRADE!

Author’s Notes

After several of my puzzle ideas failed in production, I was writing an essay about the caecilian (weird amphibians; look them up!) fossil record and I wanted to procrastinate. Checking our puzzle hunt planning discord I saw that someone had posted an image that initially looked like a geology- or paleontology- themed puzzle, but that turned out to be randomly-generated. How disappointing. The majority of this puzzle was written that evening after nobody could source me an actual puzzle about paleontology – at first I was hoping to have a cycle of Lagerstätte and fossil animals that are uniquely found in them (like Archaeopteryx to Solnhofen, Opabinia to Burgess Shale etc) but unfortunately that was too constrained.

I struggled to clue the Lagerstätte in such a way that people unfamiliar with them could uniquely identify each, and ended up with the present combination of listing what creatures were found there and some supplementary wordplay and/or location information. It turns out that the wiki pages on many of them are seriously lacking in information, so I tried to use the most famous and well-documented sites, but it was still a bit of a struggle. I ended up editing Wikipedia to add information for several of the locations after testsolvers were (understandably) reluctant to look elsewhere for key information. Anyways, hopefully you identified the exceptional creatures first, and didn’t just jump straight to finding the sites without knowing you were restricted to Lagerstätte – that’s doing the puzzle on hard mode!

Finally, I know the final answer phrase is a bit terrible. I couldn’t get IT IS TOP GRADE to work with the Lagerstätte, or some large number of other variant phrases. Eventually it was decided to give the dashes and box, so I hope that helped you not get stuck at the very end.

Personally I enjoy puzzles that require you to learn something new or dive into an obscure field you’ve never seen before -- I think it’s a nice break from some of the more traditional puzzle forms. I know that’s not everybody’s jam, but I hope that looking up the crazy animals was fun, and that you enjoyed learning a little bit about the wide range of creatures in our planet’s history :)
