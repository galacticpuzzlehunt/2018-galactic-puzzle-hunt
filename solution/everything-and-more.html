Solution: Everything and More
Answer: MUESLI

Written by Brian Chen

The puzzle consists of a list of eight sequences of clues, and eight enumerations at the end. Once you start solving the clues, you should notice that a lot of rare letters appear frequently.

As suggested by the title, each of the eight items solves to a sequence of words or abbreviations that contains all the letters of the alphabet (a pangram). The fact that all the clues are run together without any punctuation in-between is meant to evoke the way pangrams are usually full sentences rather than just a collection of words. Furthermore, no letter appears three or more times in each item. These constraints should help disambiguate any remaining uncertain answers, and some of the more obscure words can also be found by consulting a list of pangrams. The answers are given below. If you look at the letters that appear twice in each item, you find that all the first occurrences of each letter occur before all the second occurrences of each letter, and they appear in the same order, which spells out a word (twice):

  1. SOCK ZIMB JULY QAT HEX VFD SWIM RUNG PLATED → SIMULATED
  2. ZUMBA PYX IQ GROW VELDT JUMP NICK FRESH → UMPIRE
  3. FORD VAT CHI JYNX GEM SFW BLACK QUIZ PNG → FACING
  4. ZARQUON BMW JACKS VEXT GRIM PHD FLY → ARM
  5. MOP WIND QBERT ZAGS LUCY MXV IN JERK HALF → MINERAL
  6. VIM COD HERTZ PLAN MY FOX DW BEGS QR JUNK → MODERN
  7. FAX QUICK DRY SELF MON JAB ZIP WRY HGTV → FAIRY
  8. WAXY MJ VERB POND QUACKS GLHF MEN DITZ → AMEND

These words don’t fit in the bottom enumerations, but they can be treated as single-word clues for words that do. These clues are fairly ambiguous, but they can be solved by relying on the fact that their answers should satisfy the same pangrammatic property (notably, QUARTZ for its Q and Z):

MOCK JUDGE VS LIMB QUARTZ NEW SYLPH FIX

By the same mechanic, the duplicate letters in these answers spell out the final answer, MUESLI.

Author’s Notes

This was one of the first puzzle ideas I submitted, but one of the last to be assigned an answer (which had previously been assigned to one of my other puzzle ideas, which then became The Answer to This Puzzle Is....) Originally I envisioned cluing an entire sentence and then enumerating the word lengths in the entire sentence, but it quickly became clear that, under the restrictions of where the fixed letters had to be, it was prohibitively hard to make reasonable sentences, especially sentences that could be reasonably clued to leave no ambiguity as to the order of equal-length words. There were too many nouns and not enough other parts of speech; getting verb agreement to work was also very difficult. Still, you may be able to see some vestiges of my attempting to do this in the arrangement of words.

The ambiguity of the final step was also higher than I expected. During testsolving, our testsolvers tried FAKE, GYPSUM, and NYMPH, among other words, but eventually managed to error-correct enough to find the answer, so I thought it was fine. However, during the hunt we further discovered that a site suggested OP for the clue “Facing (abbr.)”. In addition, I realized that the mistake of having NYMPH instead of SYLPH was particularly hard to error-correct, since these words shared three letters, and the two letters in SYLPH but not NYMPH were letters that appeared elsewhere and were meant to be extracted. As a result, if solvers did not realize that letters only appeared once or twice or that the repeated letters intended for extraction appeared in the same order both times, then they might not detect this as an error. A fair number of teams guessed IMMUNE, which could be obtained by anagramming the letters that appeared more than once, if you had NYMPH instead of SYLPH. It certainly didn’t help that NYMPH was a considerably more common word. To teams who got stuck here, apologies for not catching this during testsolving. (Fun fact: this is not the first time “NYMPH” has gotten me into trouble; in an early draft of one of my first “published” puzzlehunt puzzles, A Puzzle Consisting Entirely of Random Anagrams from the 2015 MIT Mystery Hunt, I wrote a clue I thought fairly specifically clued NAIAD that all the testsolvers solved as NYMPH. But unlike here, that clue was scrapped.)

There was some debate about what enumeration to give Q*BERT. I left the asterisk in because this was an intro round puzzle and the testsolvers found it amusing.
