Solution: Trigger
Answer: THE NEW YORK TIMES

Written by Yannick Yao
Graphic idea by Leon Zhou

The puzzle is presented as a number of clues, each of which corresponds to an (infinitely deep) underground vault with a few of them having levels above ground as well. After solving some of the more obvious clues (e.g. COSPLAY, SINEWY, COSMETIC, TANGERINE), we may begin to notice that the words all begin with the trig functions SIN, COS, or TAN, which is confirmed by the title (TRIGger) and the flavortext (“SECret COTs”). In this light other clues will be more readily solvable.

The second step is to figure out how to fill in the levels beneath the clues. The fact that the vaults are infinite and only TAN words have levels above ground hints at converting these words into decimals using the trig functions (like COS(PLAY)). Interpreting the letters following the trig functions as variables and using the usual substitution A=1, B=2, …, we may take the product of the letters (“in production”) and interpret the product in radians (“pretty rad”) for the purpose of our computation. The grey levels give a one-digit or two-digit number for each vault, all of which are in the range 1~26 (underlined in the decimal expansion above). So we use the usual translation to convert them back to letters, which gives THE NEW YORK TIMES as the final answer.

Author’s Notes

The inspiration for this puzzle is the well-known math joke sin(gerine)/cos(gerine)=tangerine, which made me wonder if these values can actually be computed. Taking product seems to be a natural way to convert the suffix into a number, and the irrationality of trigonometric functions gives a large pool of digits to extract from. Following through this train of thought produced this puzzle.

The pool of words is somewhat limited, and there are no words that begin with CSC (cosecant), so I had to settle with using the three most common ones instead of using all six. Partly as a consequence, using radian is not a design choice but rather forced. Since many words have product that is divisible by 90 (e.g. any word that includes E and R), using degrees will leave too few words to build a puzzle from.
